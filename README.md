# Dispatches from the Record: Interlude — Federalism and the Person Held by the Whole

## What is this?

This is an interlude in the *Dispatches from the Record* series. It is not Clerk Notes No. 13 and not the full Federalism note. It pauses before that note to test a proposed structural definition of Federalism.

## Why should I care?

Federalism is often taught as a contest among governments: federal power, state power, local authority, preemption, anti-commandeering, state sovereignty, and civil-rights enforcement. This interlude asks a different starting question: what happens to the human being as public power changes scale?

## What is the claim?

The working claim is that Federalism may be the constitutional discipline by which one civic body preserves many real nested systems while carrying the human person across every scale as a full member of the whole.

## What is this not?

This is not the final Federalism note, legal advice, a replacement for federalism doctrine, or a claim that hard cases are easy. It does not decide incorporation, preemption, anti-commandeering, state sovereignty, civil-rights enforcement, sovereign immunity, spending-power conditions, dormant Commerce Clause questions, or any other doctrine.

## How was this done?

The interlude tests the proposed definition against incorporation, anti-commandeering, preemption, state sovereignty, civil-rights enforcement, states’ rights, national power, and structural failure signatures. It keeps the question open rather than closing the theory prematurely.
