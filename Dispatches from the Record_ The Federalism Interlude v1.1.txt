Dispatches from the Record: Interlude
Federalism and the Person Held by the Whole
Why I’m Pausing
I was preparing to write the next Clerk Note on Federalism.
Then Federalism got bigger than the note.
That happens sometimes in this series. A doctrine starts as a legal concept I want to understand, and then the structure underneath it begins to move. Standing did that. Due Process did that. Mootness did that. Equal Protection did that.
Federalism may be doing it more strongly than any of them.
I do not think this interlude is the Federalism note. It is not the final theory. It is not legal advice. It is not a replacement for federalism doctrine, incorporation doctrine, preemption doctrine, anti-commandeering doctrine, state-sovereignty doctrine, or civil-rights enforcement doctrine. It is not a claim that the hard cases are easy.
It is an invitation.
There are a small number of people who seem to be following these Dispatches closely enough to download them soon after they appear. I do not know who you are. I do not know why you are reading. But I am going to assume the charitable version: you are curious.
So before I publish the fuller Federalism note, I want to put the live question in front of you.
What if Federalism is not mainly about which government wins?
What if the better question is:
What happens to the human being as public power changes scale?
That is the question I am working through now.
The Definition I Am Testing
Here is the proposed definition.
Federalism may be the constitutional discipline by which one civic body preserves many real nested systems while carrying the human person across every scale as a full member of the whole.
That is a mouthful, so let me say it more plainly.
A state can act on you. A city can act on you. A federal agency can act on you. A court can act on you. A school board, licensing board, police department, benefits office, zoning authority, prison system, election office, or administrative agency can act on you.
But none of those systems should be able to act as though you have left the whole constitutional body.
That is the intuition.
The United States is one constitutional civic corpus. But “the whole” does not simply mean “the federal government.” The whole is the constitutional order that holds the person across scale. It is not flat. It contains states, local governments, courts, agencies, offices, districts, public systems, and many other lawful nested structures. Those systems are real. Their boundaries matter. Their authority is not imaginary.
But neither are they whole constitutional bodies unto themselves.
The person moves through them while remaining held by the larger constitutional whole.
That may be what Federalism is trying to manage.
The Human as the Translation Object
The phrase I keep returning to is this:
The person is what Federalism has to carry across scale.
Federalism is often taught as a structure of governments: federal power, state power, local authority, reserved powers, supremacy, preemption, sovereign immunity, and so on.
That is not wrong. But if we start only there, Federalism can sound like a fight among governments.
Who gets to act? Who gets to block? Who gets to command? Who gets to resist? Who gets the final word?
Those questions matter. But they are not the whole problem.
From the person’s side, Federalism is not first experienced as an org chart. It is experienced as movement through nested public systems.
You are not only “in America.” You are also in a state. You may be in a county, city, school district, police jurisdiction, tax district, court system, benefits system, licensing system, prison system, immigration system, or public health system.
Each layer can matter.
Each layer can protect. Each layer can burden. Each layer can fail. Each layer can pass responsibility to another layer.
So the question becomes:
Does the human being remain continuous across those layers?
Or does the person get translated downward into “only local,” “only state,” “only administrative,” “only procedural,” “only someone else’s responsibility”?
That is the danger.
If the person is the translation object across scale, then Federalism is not only about dividing public power. It is about preserving the human being as a full member of the constitutional whole while public power changes level.
That is the thought I want to test.
If That Is Right, Incorporation Looks Different
One old Federalism question asks why national constitutional protections should bind state governments.
In ordinary terms:
If a state acts on you, are you only inside the state’s system?
Or are you still held by the whole constitutional order?
If the definition I am testing is right, the answer is: you remain held by the whole.
The state is real. Its authority is real. Its boundary matters.
But the state does not become the entire civic body merely because it is the public actor nearest to you.
That means constitutional protections do not simply vanish when the immediate actor is state or local rather than federal. The person does not leave the whole corpus when passing into a nested system.
So, structurally, incorporation starts to look less like federal intrusion and more like whole-corpus continuity.
The question becomes:
What protections must travel with the person through every nested public system?
That is one way the definition might change the old argument.
It does not decide every case. It does not settle every doctrinal line. It does not replace the Fourteenth Amendment path by which many federal constitutional protections apply against the states. But it changes the starting picture.
Instead of asking only, “Why is the federal Constitution reaching into state authority?” we can also ask:
How could the state reach the person as if the whole constitutional body were absent?
If That Is Right, Anti-Commandeering Looks Different
Another old Federalism question asks why the federal government can bind states in some ways but cannot simply command state officials to administer federal programs.
In ordinary terms:
If the nation is one constitutional body, why can’t the national layer just use state systems as its instruments?
The answer may be: because nested systems have to remain real.
If a state is a real nested civic system, then its boundary cannot be decorative. Its officers, procedures, institutions, and internal authority surfaces cannot simply become federal machinery whenever the national layer wants a local tool.
That would flatten the system.
It would preserve national power by erasing the nested system that Federalism is supposed to keep real.
So, structurally, anti-commandeering starts to look less like anti-national obstruction and more like a protection of scale.
The whole corpus is real. The nested system is real. Neither one gets to pretend the other is fake.
The question becomes:
When does national coordination become federal flattening?
That is a different way to hear the doctrine.
It does not mean the national layer is weak. It means the national layer cannot preserve the whole by destroying the lawful reality of the nested systems inside it. That structural point sits beside, rather than replaces, the legal doctrine that limits federal commandeering of state governments and state officials.
If That Is Right, Preemption Looks Different
Preemption asks when federal law overrides state law.
That question can sound like a simple hierarchy:
Federal wins. State loses.
Sometimes that is the right legal result. But structurally, the issue may be more precise.
If the constitutional body is one body with many nested systems, then multiple layers may sometimes reach the same person, conduct, market, institution, or legal surface. When that happens, the system needs a way to preserve coherence.
Preemption may be one of those ways.
It is not always domination. Sometimes, structurally, it is the whole corpus saying:
These layers cannot impose incompatible commands on the same surface.
The person, actor, institution, or market cannot be held in two conflicting ways at once without the civic body losing coherence.
So preemption may be whole-corpus coordination.
But there is a danger on the other side.
If preemption becomes too broad, too casual, or too eager, it can flatten the nested systems that Federalism is supposed to preserve. The national layer can harden into control, leaving state and local systems unable to perform lawful local functions.
Legally, preemption still depends on constitutional and statutory doctrine, including the specific federal law at issue. Structurally, that is why the question cannot be only:
Does federal law win?
The deeper question is:
Is preemption preserving coherence, or is it locking the nested systems out of their lawful work?
That is the Federalism tension.
If That Is Right, State Sovereignty Looks Different
State sovereignty is one of the hardest Federalism phrases because it can mean very different things depending on how it is used.
Sometimes it names something real.
If states are genuine nested civic systems, then they must have real internal authority. A real boundary has an inside face. The state must be able to govern, administer, legislate, adjudicate, tax, regulate, and maintain public order within its lawful sphere.
If that inside face is denied entirely, the state is not a nested system. It is only a local office of the national layer.
So state sovereignty matters.
It is not fake. But it cannot be absolute.
A state is not a separate whole body. It exists inside the constitutional corpus. Its boundary is real, but it is not a wall against the person’s membership in the whole.
That means state sovereignty has to be heard in two possible ways.
It can be a lawful boundary claim:
This nested system has real authority and cannot be erased.
Or it can become boundary capture:
This nested system is using its real boundary to block the person’s claim on the whole constitutional body.
Those are not the same.
The question becomes:
When is state sovereignty a lawful boundary, and when is it boundary capture?
That distinction may be one of the most important things this definition gives us.
If That Is Right, Civil-Rights Enforcement Looks Different
Civil-rights enforcement often raises the inverse question.
When may the national layer intervene against state or local failures?
If Federalism is only a fight between governments, the question can sound like intrusion.
Why is the national government entering state space?
But if the person remains held by the whole constitutional corpus at every scale, the question changes.
When a nested system fails the person-boundary, national action may be whole-corpus repair.
A state may be real. A local system may be real. A police department, school district, court system, prison system, or agency may be real.
But none of them should be able to injure, misrecognize, exclude, silence, or degrade the person as though the whole constitutional body has no claim on the matter.
So, structurally, civil-rights enforcement may be the whole body responding when a nested system cannot or will not preserve the person as a full member of the whole.
That does not mean every national intervention is justified. The national layer must still satisfy the legal authority, facts, limits, and remedies required for the intervention it chooses.
Repair can become occupation. Protection can become flattening. Whole-body response can become national overreach.
But the opposite failure is just as real.
Local authority can become a place where the person disappears from the whole.
The question becomes:
When does whole-corpus repair preserve the person without flattening the local system?
That may be the Federalism question hiding inside many civil-rights fights.
If That Is Right, “States’ Rights” Splits in Two
The phrase “states’ rights” can carry very different meanings.
Sometimes it names a real constitutional concern.
A state may be preserving lawful local authority. It may be protecting a nested system from being flattened by national power. It may be insisting that local conditions, local institutions, local voters, local lawmaking, and local responsibility are not fake.
That can be legitimate.
But “states’ rights” can also mean something very different.
It can be a boundary word used to block the person’s claim on the whole constitutional body. It can become a way for a nested system to say:
The whole corpus cannot reach this person here.
That is not lawful boundary preservation. That is boundary capture.
The proposed definition helps split the phrase.
It asks:
Is the state preserving a real nested system?
Or is it using its nested boundary to keep the person from being held by the whole?
That question matters because Federalism should not be reduced to slogans.
“States’ rights” is not automatically noble. “States’ rights” is not automatically corrupt. It depends on what the boundary is doing.
The structural question is:
Is the state preserving a lawful boundary, or blocking the person’s claim on the whole?
That is the difference.
If That Is Right, National Power Splits in Two
The same is true in the other direction.
National power can preserve the whole constitutional body.
It can protect shared membership. It can prevent local exclusion. It can preserve national markets, national rights, national memory, national rule, and national coherence. It can act when nested systems fail, fragment, discriminate, or trap persons inside local power.
That can be necessary.
But national power can also flatten.
It can treat states, local governments, institutions, and communities as if their boundaries are not real. It can centralize too much. It can make local systems into instruments. It can erase the local perception, local responsibility, and local variation that nested systems exist to preserve.
So national power also splits.
It can be whole-corpus coherence.
Or it can become central flattening.
The proposed definition asks:
Is the national layer preserving the whole?
Or is it pretending it is the whole?
That is the difference.
A healthy Federalism cannot be built from automatic suspicion of national power.
It also cannot be built from automatic trust in national power.
The question is scale-specific:
Is national action preserving the person across the nested systems, or erasing the systems that help carry the person locally?
That is not a slogan for or against national power. It is a scale question. 
If That Is Right, Federalism Failure Has Names
One reason Federalism can be hard to talk about is that its failures are often flattened into one argument.
Overreach. Obstruction. Centralization. States’ rights. National supremacy. Local control. Federal intrusion.
Those words may be useful, but they do not always tell us what failed.
If Federalism is about one civic body, many nested systems, and the person held by the whole at every scale, then its failures may have different shapes. These are not doctrinal elements, pleading requirements, or substitutes for federalism analysis. They are structural failure patterns that help explain why different federalism failures feel different.
Dissolution happens when the nested systems stop carrying as one body.
The civic corpus fragments. State, local, national, or institutional systems begin acting as if they are not nested inside a shared constitutional order at all.
Shear happens when the person falls between layers.
One layer has power. Another has responsibility. Another has accountability. Another has the excuse. The human signal loses an owner.
Drift happens when a layer forgets its scale.
National authority drifts into central flattening. State or local authority drifts into total sovereignty. A lawful layer starts acting as if it were the whole.
Echo happens when responsibility bounces between systems.
Every layer hears the problem, but each routes it elsewhere. The person keeps speaking, but the signal circulates instead of resolving.
Lock happens when one layer freezes another.
The national layer may harden so strongly that nested systems cannot perform lawful local work. Or a state or local layer may harden so strongly that whole-corpus obligations cannot reach the person. In either direction, a real layer stops acting like a layer and starts acting like a wall.
These are not five ways of saying the same thing.
They are different kinds of Federalism failure.
And that distinction matters.
If a person is abandoned between systems, that is not the same as a state preserving a lawful boundary.
If a national rule preserves whole-body coherence, that is not the same as central flattening.
If local authority protects real local response, that is not the same as boundary capture.
So the question becomes:
Which failure are we actually seeing?
What This Interlude Is Not
This interlude is not the Federalism note.
It is not a finished theory of Federalism.
It is not legal advice.
It does not decide incorporation, preemption, anti-commandeering, state sovereignty, civil-rights enforcement, sovereign immunity, spending-power conditions, dormant Commerce Clause questions, or any other doctrine.
It does not claim the hard cases are easy.
It is a pause before the note.
I am sharing the definition I am testing because the question feels worth putting in public before I finish the fuller treatment.
The definition may hold. It may need revision. It may break somewhere important. It may reveal more than I currently see.
That is the point of the invitation.
The Invitation
So here is the question for anyone thinking along with this series:
If Federalism is the constitutional discipline by which one civic body preserves many real nested systems while carrying the human person across every scale as a full member of the whole, what follows — and where does that definition break?
Try it against an old Federalism argument.
Try it against incorporation.
Try it against state sovereignty.
Try it against civil-rights enforcement.
Try it against preemption.
Try it against anti-commandeering.
Try it against whatever doctrine first comes to mind when you hear the word Federalism.
Ask:
Does this definition make the problem clearer?
Does it explain too much?
Does it explain too little?
Does it hide something important?
Does it break?
Does it show why two doctrines that seemed opposed might actually be protecting different faces of the same structure?
Does it help you see what happens to the human being as power changes scale?
That last question is the one I keep returning to.
Because if the human person is the object Federalism must carry across scale, then Federalism is not only a doctrine about governments.
It is a doctrine about the person moving through nested public systems without losing membership in the whole.
That may be the key.
Or it may be the first step toward a better key.
Either way, it is worth testing.
Closing Thought
Before I publish the full Federalism note, I want to leave the question open.
Maybe Federalism is not mainly about which government wins.
Maybe it is about how one constitutional civic body remains one without becoming flat.
Maybe it is about how states, local systems, courts, agencies, and federal institutions remain real without becoming separate constitutional bodies.
Maybe it is about how the person remains held by the whole while public power changes scale.
If that is right, the old question changes.
It is no longer only:
Which government gets to act?
It becomes:
What happens to the human being as power changes scale?
That is the question I am taking into the Federalism note.
And if you are one of the people reading these Dispatches as they arrive, I invite you to take it there too.
